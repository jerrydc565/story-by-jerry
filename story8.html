The Wisdom Pot

I n a village that straddled a great river lived two brothers, Nduka the elder and Chidi the younger. When their father, the village sage, grew old, he called them to his bedside and showed them a mysterious clay pot. "Inside this pot," he whispered, "lies all the wisdom I have gathered in my lifetime. One of you will inherit it, but first, you must prove yourselves worthy. Take the pot to the top of the Wisdom Hill beyond the river, without opening it. The one who succeeds will not only gain the pot but become the next village sage." The next morning, Nduka, being the eldest, took the first attempt. He strapped the pot to his back and began his journey. But curiosity overwhelmed him halfway up the hill. "Surely," he thought, "I could peek inside and use some of the wisdom to help me climb better." The moment he opened the pot, all the wisdom escaped in a great gust of wind, scattering like leaves across the valley. Ashamed, Nduka returned home. Now it was Chidi's turn. But instead of trying to climb the hill, he sat by the river for three days, watching how the birds built their nests and how the ants carried heavy loads together. On the fourth day, he began his journey, but not before asking the village children to join him, singing and telling stories as they climbed. When they reached the top, Chidi discovered that the pot was empty. His father, who had followed them secretly, emerged smiling. "The true wisdom," he said, "was not in the pot at all, but in learning how to climb the hill. Nduka sought wisdom for himself alone, but you, Chidi, knew that wisdom grows when shared with others." From that day forward, Chidi became known as a wise leader who never kept knowledge to himself but shared it freely with all who wished to learn.

Moral: True wisdom lies not in what we keep to ourselves, but in what we learn and share with others.
