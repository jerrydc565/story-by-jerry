The Moon's Promise

I n ancient times, before electricity lit up our nights, the Moon had a special promise to the children of Earth. Each month, she would come down from the sky, disguised as an old woman, to check if children were keeping their traditions and respecting their elders. One such night, in a small village, only a young girl named Olayinka was awake, unable to sleep because of hunger. Her family had fallen on hard times after a poor harvest. As she sat outside her hut, she saw an old woman struggling with a heavy bag. Despite her own hunger and fatigue, Olayinka rushed to help the old woman. She offered her a seat, brought her water from the stream, and even shared her last piece of dried cassava. As they talked, Olayinka told stories she had learned from her grandmother, sang traditional songs, and showed the old woman how she was learning to weave raffia like her mother had taught her. The old woman smiled, revealing teeth that glowed like starlight. "I am the Moon," she said, "and you have shown me that the old ways still live in the hearts of children." She reached into her bag and pulled out a handful of shining beads. "Plant these in your family's farm when the night is darkest," she instructed. "But remember - you must sing the old songs while planting them." Olayinka did as she was told, singing the traditional farming songs her grandmother had taught her. By the next full moon, the beads had grown into plants bearing fruits and vegetables of silver and gold. But more precious than the magical crops was the lesson that spread through the village: keeping traditions alive brings its own kind of magic. From that day forward, during every full moon, parents would tell their children to look up at the Moon's bright face and remember her promise - that she watches over those who remember their roots and help others, even in times of need.

Moral: Our traditions and kindness are like seeds that, when nurtured, can grow into unexpected blessings.
